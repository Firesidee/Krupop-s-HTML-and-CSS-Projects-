Facts

Domestic Chickens (Gallus domesticus) have been around for thousands of years
and are descendants of are descendants of Red Junglefowl (Gallus gallus). They've
served many purposes, from supplying food with their meat and eggs, to aiding fishermen
who use their feathers to make fly ties. Many hobbyists also enjoy breeding for certain
qualities and competing with others in poultry shows.

The earliest indications of domestic chickens existing are skeletal remains found in China,
dated back to 5400 B.C. As it so happens, Marco Polo was the first to record "Chickens with
fur instead of feathers" (Silkies)

Common Breeds

There are literally hundreds of modern domestic chickens breeds, so to save
you the headache of looking through each breed, trying to decide what breed might
be best for you, I've listed a few of the most common breeds along with their facts
below.

Easter Egger

Hen

The Easter Egger is not an official breed, seeing as they have no breed standard. However, they are
very popular, mainly due to the fact that the hens lay blue or green eggs and that they can be bought at
a much cheaper price than their recognized breed descendants, the Ameraucana.

Even though these guys are not a breed, it does not mean they should be passed by.
The hens are very sweet an gentle, easily tameable, and, like most chickens, will begin
to lay eggs at around six months of age.

Fun Fact: Due to the fact that Easter Eggers were used to create Ameraucanas, and
Ameraucanas are more desirable by breeders/show hobbyists, a lot of feed stores will sell
Easter Egger chicks labeled as Ameraucanas to unsuspecting customers. The best way to avoid
this is to buy chicks from honest breeders, or to look closely at how the store spells Ameraucana,
as most will misspell it, instead writing something like "Americana" on the bin breed tag.

Buff Orpington

The Buff Orpington is a breed of chicken that is very calm and great with beginners, while are popular
among egg enthusiasts due to the fact that they boast many feathers for a very fluffy appearance and feel,
as well as the fact that hens lay a light brown egg. This breed is also popular among show hobbyists, with
show birds having a more rounded appearance than hatchery birds.

Fun Fact: The "Buff" Orpington isn't a breed in and of itself. Buff is the variety, (color)
Orpington is the breed. Orpingtons can come in several varieties such as Lavender, Jubilee
and White.

Black Sex-link

Once again, these birds are not actually a breed, but nevertheless remain popular among egg hobbyists and
people who cannot own roosters due to zoning laws. They are a cross-breed between a barred hen and a black
rooster of any breed. All hatchery/feed store chicks will lay brown eggs, but can lay blue/green eggs if a
breeder puts a black Ameraucana rooster over a barred or cuckoo hen since the blue egg gene is dominant over
white or brown egg genes. This is a great beginner bird.

Fun fact: Due to the fact that barred/cuckoo hens can only produce barred roosters and black hens when bred to a black
rooster, the chicks can be sexed at hatching, resulting in providing the buyer with no risk of an accidental rooster
in a hen only environment.

Sexing

Sexing is relatively easy, especially once you get the hang of it. You may hear of a thing called "Feather
Sexing" don't rely on it. It only works with chicks that have fast growing feathers and is not very accurate.

Unless the chick is a sex-at-hatch breed (Black Sex-link, Bielefelder, Red/Gold Sex-link, Crested Cream Legbar)
chicks cannot be effecientally sexed until 4-6 weeks of age unless the breed is a difficult to sex one, such as
a silkie. Those cannot be effecientally sexed until 8-12 weeks.

The most effective way for sexing chicks at this age is to look at the comb, most pullets will have a small yellow
or pink comb, while a cockeral will have a larger red comb with waddles already beginning to develop.

If you are sexing young bird that are 12 weeks or older, you can use the saddle feather method. Saddle feathers are the
feathers that grow down the chicken's back and hang down just in front of the tail. This is a very reliable sexing method, really the only way
it can fail is if you are dealing with silkies or a breed with "hennie" feathering. Hennie feathering is a gene that causes
roosters to display hen-like saddle feathers for their entire lives. (Silver Sebrights) Normally, roosters will have long, slender,
pointy saddle feathers and hens will have short, wide, and round saddle feathers.
